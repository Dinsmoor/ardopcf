If you see a bright pink box at the top of the page saying 'Connection to ardopcf lost. Try reloading this page.', then this page is unable to establish a connection with ardopcf, or that connection has been closed. This can occur for a number of reasons. This will be displayed if the ardopcf program is terminated (intentionally or not). If you are displaying this page on a device (computer, table, phone, etc.) other that the one that is running the ardopcf program, then this can be caused by a problem with the network (often WiFi) connection between them. It is normal for this message to very briefly flash at the top of this page when it first loads.

A few different devices can view copies of this Web GUI for that same running ardopcf instance. However, ardopcf will refuse additional connections if too many copies try to connect simultaneously.

The top of this page shows the current 'ProtocolMode'. This will be one of 'ARQ', 'FEC', or 'RXO'. Next is shown the current Ardop protocol 'State' This may be:

'DISC'
disconnected
'FECRcv'
receiving Forward Error Correction (FEC) frame
'FECSend'
sending FEC frame
'ISS'
station is the Information Sending Station
'IRS'
station is the Information Receiving Station
'IRStoISS'
transition from 'IRS' to 'ISS'
'IDLE'
idle

The next blank line will show 'PTT' when transmitting and 'Channel Busy' when the busy detector detects a signal within Ardop's current bandwidth settings.

The next line shows the value of MYCALL and the callsign of the station you are connected to or sending Connection Requests (ConReq) to. MYCALL is normally set by the Host program such as Pat Winlink or gARIM. This should be your callsign. If it has not been set, then it will show '(MYCALL NOT SET)'.

The spectrum and waterfall plots show from about 300 to 2700 Hz. If this is narrower than the receive filter of your radio, high and/or low pitched audio that you hear from your radio may not be visible on these plots. The thin black line in the center of the waterfall display is at 1500 Hz, the center frequency for Ardop signals. The gray or pink lines on each side of the plots indicate the aproximate edges of the currently selected ARQ bandwidth (200, 500, 1000, or 2000 Hz wide). These lines are not labeled, but their location relative to the total bandwidth make it obvious which of these bandwidths they represent.

The levels shown in the spectrum and waterfall displays are log scale (dB), and a very slow automatic gain control is used to try to clearly show the relative power levels of signals and noise detected in the available audio. User adjustable 'Plot Averaging' may optionally be applied. Averaging smooths out the appearance of noise, which may make it easier to distinguish low power signals. However, it also makes the plots less responsive to quickly changing received signals. If the busy detector fails to recognize a signal that you can see or hear within the Ardop bandwidth you intend to use, you should not attempt to establish a connection at this time on this frequency, because you may interfere with the existing signal.

'Plot Scale' may be used to increase the size of all of the plots. This does not change the amount of data being used for the plots, it just magnifies them.

Scaling of data for plotting, include averaging, does NOT affect the 'Rcv Level' indicator, the busy detector, or ardopcf's abilility to detect or decode data. These adjustments are purely for display purposes.

The constellation plot provides a visual indicator of the quality of the most recently received PSK/QAM frame. The display shown for FSK frames provides less information, but may still be somewhat useful.

The 'Rcv Level' graph shows the percentage of full scale used by the received audio on a linear scale. If this level is too high, the audio will be clipped, making it harder for ardopcf to decode received signals. The 'Rcv Level' indicates the combined amplitude of everything in your radio's receive bandwidth, and is thus not influenced by the Ardop bandwidth settings indicated on the spectrum and waterfall plots. A strong signal outside of the Ardop bandwidth can still cause audio clipping.

A warning message is shown when excessive audio levels are detected. This message will disappear a few seconds after signal levels drop to more reasonable levels. Low to moderate signal levels seem to usually work well. Audio levels that are too low may also make it more difficult for ardopcf to decode received signals. A warning message is shown when received audio is very low. However, if noise levels are low, the 'Low Audio' warning may be ignored when no signals are present. If the 'Low Audio' warning remains on when ardop signals are being received, then increasing the audio level may improve decoding. Increasing your received audio level may also help if you see faint indication of possible ardop signals centered on the waterfall display, but ardopcf is not decoding these signals.

Received audio levels are dependent on the strength of the received signal, on your radio's settings as well as on the 'microphone' settings for your computer's soundcard. Details of how to make these adjustments depend on the model of radio you are using and on what operating system your computer uses.

The 'RX Frame Type' and 'TX Frame Type' displays show what Ardop frame is currently being received or sent respectively. While receiving, the 'RX Frame Type' is initially shown with a yellow background. If this frame is not successfully decoded, the background will change to red. If it is successfully decoded, the background will change to green. The 'TX Frame Type' is normally shown with a white background, but a green background is used when sending a DataACK frame telling the other station that you correctly received some data. A red background is used when sending a DataNAK frame telling the other station that you were unable to decode some received data.

The 'TX DriveLevel' slider linearly scales the amplitude of the audio sent to your radio by ardopcf. This, along with your radio's settings and the 'speaker' settings for your computer's soundcard influence the strength and quality of your transmitted radio signal. This slider control will always be set to to 100 when ardopcf is first started. You may want to adjust your radio and soudcard settings to levels that allow you to use this convenient slider control to make final adjustments to this audio output level.

Reduced audio amplitude can be used to decrease the power of your transmitted signals. In addition to limiting your power to only what is required to carry out the desired communications [for US Amateur operator this is required by Part 97.313(a)], reducing your power output may also be necessary if your radio cannot handle transmitting at its full rated power when using high duty cycle digital modes. While sending data, Ardop can have a very high duty cycle as it sends long data frames with only brief breaks to hear short DataACK or DataNAK frames from the other station.

If the output audio is too loud, your radio's Automatic Level Control (ALC) will adjust this audio before using it to modulate the RF signal. This adjustment may distort the signal making it more difficult for other stations to correctly receive your transmissions.

Details of how to monitor the action of your radio's ALC, and how to adjust your radio and soundcard settings depend on the model of radio you are using and on what operating system your computer uses.

The 'Send2Tone' button causes ardopcf to transmit a 5 second two tone signal similar, but longer than what is used as the leader at the start of all Ardop frames. This will not be sent if the Protocol 'STATE', as shown near the top of this page, is not 'DISC' for disconnected. An alert is displayed for this failure.

The 'SendID' button causes ardopcf to transmit an ID frame containing your callsign. If MYCALL has not been set, such that '(MYCALL NOT SET)', then this frame cannot be sent, and an alert is displayed instead. Similarly, this will not be sent if the Protocol 'STATE', as shown near the top of this page, is not 'DISC' for disconnected. An alert is displayed for this failure as well. If a Maidenhead grid square indicating your location has been set by the Host program, this is also included in an ID frame.

The various items displayed here can change quickly during an Ardop ARQ connection. The debug log file created by ardopcf contains most of this same information and more. Reviewing its content may be of interest after an exchange that failed, or during which you saw something that seemed unusual. The 'Show Log' button also provides a list of most of what has been displayed. This low window shows less detail than the debug log file, but provides a convenient way to review what has occured. Some entries in the log window are timestamped. These timestamps use the clock of the device you are viewing this page on, which may be different from the clock on the device that is running the ardopcf program. Due to this, times shown in the log window may be differnt from those written to the debug log file.

The 'Clear Log' button (visible only when the log window is visible) may be used to discard the contents of this window. This might be useful before initiating a new connection. The 'Clear Log' button does not influence what is written to the debug log file.
